\section{Analysis of the \texttt{q} file}

The analysis of the \texttt{DQD\_SiMOS\_q.vtu} output was carried out in
ParaView by cutting the device at the height of the quantum layer
(\(z=45\,\mathrm{nm}\)) and by extracting a \textit{Plot Over Line} along the
axis connecting the two dots (\(y=136.5\,\mathrm{nm}\)). The plotted quantities
are the confinement potential and the probability densities \(|\psi_i|^2\) of
the first five single-particle eigenstates.

\hline
Ground & 0.01115999 & Ground orbital of the right dot. \\
1st excited & 0.01120989 & Ground orbital of the left dot. \\
2nd excited & 0.01396172 & First excited orbital of the right dot. \\
3rd excited & 0.01400675 & First excited orbital of the left dot. \\
4th excited & 0.01438458 & Higher orbital with finite density in the barrier region. \\
\hline

\caption{First five eigenenergies obtained from the Schr\"odinger solution.}
\label{tab:q-energies}
\end{table}

For an ideal symmetric double dot, the two states belonging to the same
orbital shell should be degenerate. In the simulation they instead appear as
two close but resolved pairs. The ground-state pair is split by
\(4.99\times10^{-5}\,\mathrm{eV}=49.9\,\mu\mathrm{eV}\), while the first
excited pair is split by
\(4.50\times10^{-5}\,\mathrm{eV}=45.0\,\mu\mathrm{eV}\). These splittings are
small compared with the energy separation between the two shells,
\(E_2-E_0\simeq2.80\,\mathrm{meV}\). We therefore interpret the spectrum as
two almost degenerate dot orbitals, staggered by a small left-right asymmetry.
This staggering is already present at the level of the initial geometry/mesh,
so it is reported as a numerical/geometrical splitting of the nominally
degenerate states rather than as an additional physical conclusion from the
post-processing.

The confinement map in Fig.~\ref{fig:q-conf-slice} shows two electrostatic
minima separated by a finite central barrier. Sampling the same line used for
the wavefunction plots gives \(V_L\simeq5.03\,\mathrm{meV}\) and
\(V_R\simeq4.84\,\mathrm{meV}\) for the two dot minima, and an inter-dot saddle
of \(V_b\simeq15.83\,\mathrm{meV}\). The fourth excited level,
\(E_4=14.38\,\mathrm{meV}\), is therefore only about \(1.45\,\mathrm{meV}\)
below the barrier top. This explains why this state has a visible probability
density inside the barrier region, while the lower levels remain more strongly
confined inside the individual wells.

The density slices in Fig.~\ref{fig:q-density-slices} confirm the assignment
of the line profiles. The two lowest states are single-lobe states localized
in opposite wells, with a much weaker residual density in the other dot. This
residual component is expected for a finite inter-dot barrier: the computed
eigenstates belong to the full double-well Hamiltonian, not to two isolated
dots. The small left-right staggering makes the lowest states mostly
localized; in a perfectly symmetric device the same finite tunnel coupling
would instead produce more delocalized bonding and anti-bonding combinations
over the two dots. The second and third excited states show the expected
two-lobe structure of the first excited orbital, again localized mainly on one
dot at a time. The fourth excited state is the only one among the computed
levels with an appreciable density in the region separating the two wells.

\section{Concluding remarks on the output analysis}

The generated output files describe two complementary aspects of the device.
The \texttt{n} file reports the classical carrier density obtained from the
Poisson solution. Its slice and line profile show that the central quantum
region is depleted, while the reservoirs and highly doped contact regions
remain populated. This confirms that the electrostatic gates isolate the
active region from classical drift-diffusion transport, leaving the dot region
available for the single-particle quantum analysis.

The \texttt{q} file instead contains the confinement potential and the
probability densities of the Schr\"odinger eigenstates. These results show two
well-defined potential minima, two nearly degenerate orbital pairs, and a
finite inter-dot barrier. The lowest states are mostly localized on individual
dots, but the non-zero probability density visible in the opposite dot already
indicates that the two wells are not completely independent. Since only
\(|\psi|^2\) is plotted, the relative phase between the two dot components is
not visible; however, the corresponding signed wavefunctions would be expected
to show bonding and anti-bonding combinations. This character would be more
pronounced for perfectly degenerate dots, while the observed left-right
staggering localizes the states more strongly on one dot. The fourth excited
state, whose energy lies close to the barrier saddle, has the largest density
in the inter-dot region and therefore shows the clearest hybridized character
among the computed levels.

Overall, the simulated structure behaves as expected for a gate-defined double
quantum dot: the electrostatic solution creates the required depletion and
confinement, and the quantum solution resolves localized dot states together
with the onset of tunnel-mediated hybridization. The probability-density maps
therefore support a weakly coupled double-dot configuration, with
bonding/anti-bonding behavior likely present in the signed wavefunctions but
partially masked in the plotted \(|\psi|^2\) fields by the residual
left-right staggering.
